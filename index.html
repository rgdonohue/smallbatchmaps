A Case Study in Human-AI Collaboration: Tilecraft

Tilecraft is an AI-assisted CLI tool I built that demonstrates these principles in action. It combines OpenStreetMap data processing with AI-powered schema generation and prompt-based style design. Users provide natural language descriptions like "subalpine dusk" or "urban midnight" and the system generates complete vector tile pipelines with beautiful, context-aware cartographic styles.

About Richard Donohue

Crafting Maps with Purpose

With a Ph.D. in Geography, GIS, and Cartography, I specialize in transforming complex geospatial data into meaningful insights and beautiful, functional maps. My approach combines traditional cartographic principles with modern web technologies and current AI-assisted workflows.

I believe in ethical data practices and human-centered design. Every project is treated as a unique challenge, deserving custom solutions rather than cookie-cutter approaches. Whether you need sophisticated spatial analysis, interactive web applications, or AI-assisted workflows, I bring both technical expertise and creative vision to your geospatial challenges.

Born and raised in Durango, CO, I enjoy the outdoors, hiking, and skiing.

Career Highlights

University of Kentucky: A Decade of Mentorship and Growth

Over my 10 years at the University of Kentucky, I had the privilege of mentoring over 30 MS students as a post-doctoral researcher and assistant professor. This experience honed my skills in guiding research projects, fostering academic growth, and nurturing the next generation of geospatial experts. Each student brought unique challenges and perspectives, enriching my understanding and approach to geospatial science.

Consulting for the World Health Organization

My consulting work with the World Health Organization involved developing decision support tools for pathogen outbreaks. I designed data dashboards for both public and internal use, ensuring accessibility and clarity in critical situations. Additionally, I worked on network mapping prototypes, which played a crucial role in visualizing and managing complex health data networks.
